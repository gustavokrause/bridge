# Cyan Stratigraphy

**Philosophy:** Knowledge has depth, and depth has order. Meaning is read the way a survey reads water — from a bright, legible surface down through measured strata into a quiet, pressured dark. This is a visual language of *soundings*: every mark is an instrument reading, every line a contour of something studied with patience. The movement treats an abstract system the way a 19th-century naturalist treated an ocean — as a phenomenon worthy of being charted, catalogued, and rendered with reverence. Nothing is illustrated for charm; everything is *recorded*. The result must feel like a plate pulled from a hydrographic atlas that took a master draughtsman a lifetime to engrave.

**Space and form.** Composition descends. The page is a vertical column of water, divided into horizontal strata that the eye sinks through in sequence. Each stratum is a band of its own density, separated by a single hairline — never a heavy border, only the quiet seam where one layer of pressure gives way to the next. A margin axis runs the full height like the depth scale on a real chart, ticked and numbered, so the viewer always knows how deep they have read. Negative space is not emptiness; it is water, and it carries weight. The lower a thing sits, the more space presses around it.

**Color and material.** A single cold family governs everything: the cyanotype blues of blueprint and deep water, from a luminous pale at the surface to near-black in the abyss. The palette is monochromatic by discipline, calibrated through dozens of near-identical tones so that depth itself is legible as color — light falling away, degree by degree. Into this cold order, exactly one warm signal is permitted: a thin copper current that threads through the whole composition, the single living thing in a measured sea. It must be rationed like gold leaf. Where it touches, attention follows; everywhere else, the eye rests in blue.

**Scale and rhythm.** Meaning is built by patient repetition. Contour lines accumulate into form the way isobaths accumulate into a seabed; fields of tiny identical marks gather into mass; sounding ticks march down the margin in unbroken cadence. These dense accumulations are the labor of the piece — hundreds of small, deliberate strokes that reward the sustained gaze and prove the hand behind them. Against this fine grain, scale is dramatic: one or two forms rise large and quiet, vast among the small marks, so the composition breathes between the monumental and the minute.

**Composition and hierarchy.** Order is hierarchical and one-directional, like current and like pressure. The structure should be felt before it is read: a clear top, a deep center of gravity, a settled floor. Typography is instrument-grade and sparse — thin geometric letterforms and clinical monospace, set small, behaving like the engraved annotations on a survey: catalog numbers, coordinates, a measured label beside each reading. Text never explains; it *annotates*. A single whispered line of italic may carry the soul of the piece, but the information lives in the strata, the contours, and the one warm current — not in sentences.

**Craft, repeated.** This is the heart of the movement: the work must look as though it was labored over for countless hours by someone at the absolute top of the draughtsman's craft. Every hairline must be intentional, every tick evenly spaced, every tone in the gradient deliberately placed, every alignment the product of painstaking refinement. The goal is not decoration but the meticulous, master-level execution of a scientific artifact — the kind of plate that proves something invisible can be measured, mapped, and understood through careful attention. Restraint is the final discipline: when in doubt, refine what exists until it is pristine, rather than adding one more mark. The piece is finished only when it could hang in a museum of instruments and be mistaken for a true record of the deep.
